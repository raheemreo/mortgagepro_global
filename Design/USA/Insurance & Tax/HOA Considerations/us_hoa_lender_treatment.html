🏢 What Lenders Check About the HOA
📊
Owner-Occupancy Ratio
Fannie Mae & FHA require ≥50% owner-occupied units. Buildings with too many rentals may be ineligible for conventional financing.
💸
HOA Delinquency Rate
Max 15% of units can be 30+ days delinquent on HOA dues. Higher delinquency = HOA cash-flow risk = lender rejection.
🔍
Single-Entity Ownership
No single entity can own more than 10% (Fannie) or 25% (FHA) of units. Over-concentrated ownership is a red flag.
⚖️
Active Litigation
Any active lawsuit against the HOA (construction defects, insurance disputes) can make the building ineligible for Fannie Mae/FHA financing.
🏦
Reserve Fund Adequacy
Post-Surfside, FHA requires HOAs to maintain 10%+ of annual budget in reserves. Fannie Mae conducts project reviews on high-rise condos.
🏗️
Building Condition
Lenders (especially post-2021) increasingly scrutinize structural integrity reports, especially for buildings 20+ years old or 3+ stories.
🚩 Red Flags That Kill Financing
🚨
HOA Delinquency >15%
Triggers automatic disqualification for Fannie Mae & FHA condo approval
⚖️
Active HOA Litigation
Even small lawsuits can cause "spot approval" refusals — affecting your specific unit
📉
Pending Large Special Assessment
Must be disclosed; may need to be paid in full at closing or reduce loan eligibility
🏗️
Structural Safety Concerns
Post-Surfside Florida law now requires milestone inspections; non-compliance = unlendable
🔢
Single Owner >10% of Units
Investor concentration violates Fannie Mae project eligibility requirements

💡 Pro Tip: HOA Questionnaire
Your lender will send an HOA Questionnaire to the association before closing. This captures: budget health, delinquency rate, pending litigation, owner-occupancy ratio, and reserve fund balance. Delays in HOA response are a common cause of closing delays — notify the HOA early in the process.
